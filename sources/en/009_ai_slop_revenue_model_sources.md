# 009. AI slop / Contact-Revenue Model Sources

This source note is not a comprehensive bibliography.
The materials listed here are source anchors for checking how the observed subject is explained externally, not grounds or foundations for HSS.

These source anchors are not used to prove the definition of AI slop, the quality of AI-generated music, the merits of streaming platforms, or copyright-law evaluations.
They are also not treated as authorities for proving anything about HSS.

## Source anchors

### An Empirical Analysis of AI Slop in Music Streaming

Use:
Treated as the primary source anchor for checking the framing of the AI music slop paper, low play counts, mass posting, distributors, detectors, and revenue estimates.
It is treated as an observation target for separating data from interpretation and for seeing which issues the term AI slop compresses.

### Spotify for Artists / Royalties Guide

Use:
Treated as a source anchor for checking streaming royalties, streamshare, the absence of a fixed per-stream rate, artist tools, and ticketing / merch routes.
Used to observe how playback, revenue distribution, and peripheral routes are explained on a platform.

### Spotify Loud & Clear

Use:
Treated as a source anchor for checking the streaming economy, payout flow, and platform-side explanations.
It is used not to prove the streaming-type revenue model as the normal system, but to observe how the platform explains its own processing form.

### Revenue sharing at music streaming platforms

Use:
Treated as a source anchor for checking design discussions of streaming revenue-distribution models such as pro-rata and user-centric models.
Used to observe the structure by which play counts and user units connect to revenue distribution.

### The Impact of Social Media on Music Demand

Use:
Treated as a source anchor for checking the connection among short video, SNS exposure, and streaming demand.
Used to observe that music is connected not only to the audio source itself, but also to contact and exposure on SNS.

### AI-Generated Algorithmic Virality

Use:
Treated as a source anchor for checking the connection among feeds, search, ranking, algorithmic virality, and AI-generated content.
Used to observe connection to algorithmic forms of exposure and diffusion, not to judge the value of AI-generated works themselves.

### Measuring AI “Slop” in Text

Use:
Treated as a source anchor for checking the instability of AI slop definitions and measurements.
Used as a supporting line for treating the term AI slop not as a fixed definition, but as a symbol that compresses multiple issues.

### “That’s AI Slop, You Bot!”

Use:
Treated as a source anchor for checking AI slop accusation, authenticity, gatekeeping, and label pollution.
Used to observe label pollution in which works or creators are processed in bulk merely because AI is involved.

### Why Slop Matters

Use:
Treated as a source anchor for checking arguments that affirm or valorize AI slop.
Used to avoid treating AI slop only as a simple negative term, and to observe it as a term with other connection destinations.

### Why AI Slop Matters, but Not Like That

Use:
Treated as a source anchor for checking the need for socio-technical context.
Used as a supporting line for observing connections not only to AI-generated outputs, but also to platforms, processing forms, distribution, evaluation, and revenue models.

### An Endless Stream of AI Slop

Use:
Treated as a source anchor for checking review burden, commons, confirmation load, and selection load.
Used to observe AI slop not only as output itself, but also as processing load externalized to readers and reviewers.

### Deezer / Ipsos survey and Deezer AI detection-related materials

Use:
Treated as a source anchor for checking cases where the AI-generated label connects to recommendation control, chart classification, reward distribution, platform reliability, and customer-acquisition routes.
It is not treated as grounds for evaluating the quality of AI-generated music itself.
