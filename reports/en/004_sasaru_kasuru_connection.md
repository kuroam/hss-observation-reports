# 004. The Connection Structure of “Sasaru” and “Kasuru”

## HSS Observation Report

> This report is an individual observation report using the HSS core model.
> For HSS definitions, terminology, and scope, see [hss-observation-notes](https://github.com/kuroam/hss-observation-notes).
> This report is not proof of HSS; it is a provisional observation note using HSS vocabulary as a tentative observation axis.

## 0. How this report handles the topic

This report is not a rigorous literature review of linguistics, psychology, marketing, or cultural studies.

It also does not evaluate or rank words, expressions, culture, innovation, buzz, memes, or receiver reactions.

This report keeps the Japanese terms `sasaru` and `kasuru` as HSS observation metaphors. It observes expressions such as `sasaru`, `kasuru`, `remain`, and `return later` as structures in which a symbol connects to the receiver-side layered history, reconnectable area, unease, memory, and context.

## 1. Averaged image available from external sources

In ordinary dictionary meaning, `刺さる / sasaru` refers to a pointed object sticking into something.

It is also used metaphorically for receiving a strong impact or being deeply moved.

In this report, that meaning is treated as a source anchor for observing, in HSS vocabulary, a state in which a symbol strongly connects to the receiver-side connection structure.

Meanwhile, in contexts of cultural propagation and memes, a meme is described as a cultural idea, behavior, style, or related unit that spreads through imitation.

An internet meme is also treated as a cultural unit that spreads on the internet while being shared, imitated, transformed, and reused.

Here, these are not treated as rigorous cultural theory. They are treated as source anchors for the following averaged observation object.

```text
sasaru
= a state in which a symbol strongly connects to existing layered history

kasuru
= a state in which a symbol touches the edge of a reconnectable area and remains as unease or trace

remain
= a state with possible later reconnection even without immediate fixed connection

return later
= a state in which reconnection happens later through records, media, time changes, or changes in receiver-side history
```

## 2. Points not fully decomposed by the averaged explanation

In ordinary explanations, `sasaru` may be described as an expression that receives a strong reaction, words that remain in the mind, content that gains empathy, or something that resonates.

Buzz and memes may also be explained in terms such as reacted to, shared, spread, or remained.

However, those explanations alone can make the following structural differences difficult to see.

- Is `sasaru` really a new connection?
- Which existing history does an expression that directly hits the center connect to?
- How does an expression that only `kasuru` remain as a trace?
- What changes when a connection candidate that did not sasaru at first connects later?
- How can something that does not even kasuru be handled in HSS?
- Are culture and innovation born from direct hits, or from things that touch the edge and later reconnect?

Below, this report provisionally decomposes these points using HSS vocabulary.

## 3. HSS decomposition

Seen through HSS, `sasaru` can be observed as a state in which a symbol strongly connects to the receiver-side existing layered history, memory, unease, context, or recognition form.

What matters here is that `sasaru` does not necessarily mean novelty or value.

Something that directly hits the center may be connecting with high precision to an existing thick connection route.

```text
symbol
↓
connects to existing thick layered history
↓
sasaru in the center
↓
immediate reaction, empathy, sharing, fixation
```

By contrast, `kasuru` can be observed as a state in which a symbol does not enter the center of an existing connection route, but touches the edge of a reconnectable area.

It may not be understood immediately, yet remain as unease, memory, fragment, or an unrecovered reaction.

```text
symbol
↓
touches the edge of a reconnectable area
↓
kasuru
↓
remains as unease, memory, fragment, or trace
↓
may reconnect later
```

What HSS observes is not a simple binary of “sasaru or does not sasaru.”

It observes to which receiver-side layered history a symbol connects, to what degree, and at what timing.

## 4. Can something that does not even kasuru be observed?

Something that completely does not even kasuru is almost impossible to observe in HSS.

This is because, if it connects to no receiver and remains in no record or memory, its existence disappears from the observation target.

Therefore, what this report can handle is, strictly speaking, not “something that does not even kasuru at all,” but “something that appears not to have kasuru, yet remains as some kind of trace.”

```text
completely does not even kasuru
↓
not recorded
↓
not remembered
↓
not reconnected
↓
disappears from the HSS observation target
```

On the other hand, even something that did not sasaru at the time and appears to have almost not kasuru may reconnect later through recording, preservation, media, rediscovery, time changes, or changes in receiver-side history.

```text
trace that has kasuru
↓
record / preservation / fragment
↓
media / rediscovery
↓
changes in receiver-side history or context
↓
reconnected later
```

### When it remains as a processing form inside an organization

Usually, something that completely does not even kasuru remains in no record or memory and disappears from the HSS observation target.

However, inside an organization, something that did not kasuru as a connection candidate may remain as a processing form such as a business case, ringi approval request, ticket, KPI, approval item, or explanatory material.

In this case, what remains is not reconnectability itself, but a processable form.

In HSS, this state can be observed as a phase in which something that has become hard to see as a connection candidate is preserved and circulated as an invoice.

This structure is also handled in Report 003, [Why Innovation Stops as an Invoice Bundle](003_innovation_invoice_bundle.md).

## 5. Kasuru in culture and innovation

In HSS, the early state of culture and innovation can be observed not only as something that directly hits the center, but also as something that touches the edge of a reconnectable area and remains.

Something that directly hits the center may connect easily to existing layered history, existing interests, existing context, or existing evaluation axes.

By contrast, connection candidates for culture and innovation do not necessarily enter existing thick connection routes from the beginning.

Rather, they may appear in the following states.

- Not immediately understood.
- Something catches.
- Does not fit existing classifications.
- Not completely unrelated.
- Returns later.
- Reinterpreted in another context.

In HSS, such states are observed as unfixed connection candidates that touch the edge of a reconnectable area and remain.

## 6. Decomposition result

| Observed object | State visible through HSS | Connection destination |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Direct hit / sasaru in the center | Connection to existing thick layered history | Immediate reaction, empathy, sharing, fixation |
| Slight sasaru | Connection to peripheral history | Reaction, memory, partial sharing |
| Kasuru | State of touching the edge of a reconnectable area | Unease, trace, later reconnection |
| Trace that has kasuru | Unfixed symbol that remains without disappearing | Record, preservation, rediscovery |
| Does not even kasuru | State in which no connection route is observed | Disappears from the HSS observation target |
| Sasaru later | Reconnection after changes in receiver-side history or context | Re-evaluation, culturalization, conversion to another use |
| Symbol adjusted to sasaru from the beginning | High-precision connection to existing history | Recovery into existing context, short-term propagation |

## 7. Observation hypotheses inferable from the HSS model

The following hypotheses are not conclusions directly derived from external sources. They are observation hypotheses formed from the decomposition that appears when HSS is placed as a tentative observation axis.

### Hypothesis 1: `sasaru` can be observed as connection to existing layered history

`Sasaru` can be observed as a state in which a symbol connects to the receiver-side existing layered history, memory, unease, context, or recognition form.

For that reason, `sasaru` may appear not as novelty itself, but as a high-precision connection to an existing connection route.

### Hypothesis 2: `kasuru` can be observed as an unfixed connection candidate

`Kasuru` can be observed as a state in which a symbol does not enter the center of an existing connection route, but touches the edge of a reconnectable area.

When that trace remains, it appears as an unfixed connection candidate that may reconnect later.

### Hypothesis 3: Culture may form from things that kasuru and remain

Culture may form not only from things that directly sasaru in the center, but also from things that are a little strange, remain slightly, and are picked up again by another person in another context.

In HSS, this state can be observed as a process in which a trace that has kasuru connects into layered history.

### Hypothesis 4: Innovation does not necessarily sasaru from the beginning

Connection candidates for innovation do not necessarily connect strongly to existing evaluation axes or approval forms from the beginning.

Rather, they may touch the edge of a reconnectable area and remain as unease, prototype, fragment, or hypothesis.

In HSS, this state can be observed as an unfixed connection candidate that can become reconnectable later.

### Hypothesis 5: Things adjusted to sasaru from the beginning may narrow Blue residuals

A symbol adjusted to sasaru from the beginning may connect precisely to existing history, existing interests, existing evaluation axes, or existing approval forms.

This can support short-term propagation, but it may also narrow Blue residuals by reducing what remains outside the existing route.

### Hypothesis 6: Something that does not even kasuru disappears from observation unless it remains as a trace

Something that completely does not even kasuru remains in no record or memory, so HSS handles it as disappearing from the observation target.

For that reason, HSS can handle not complete non-connection itself, but connection candidates that remain as some record, fragment, or processing form.

### Hypothesis 7: Later reconnection can happen when receiver-side history or context changes

A symbol that did not sasaru at first may reconnect later when receiver-side layered history, context, media, evaluation axes, or social conditions change.

In HSS, this can be observed not as a sudden birth of value, but as delayed reconnection through a remaining trace.

## 10. Short conclusion

`Sasaru` is not simply novelty or value.

It can be observed as a state in which a symbol strongly connects to the receiver-side existing layered history and reconnectable areas.

By contrast, `kasuru` can be observed as a state in which a symbol touches the edge of a reconnectable area and remains as unease or trace.

Connection candidates for culture and innovation do not necessarily sasaru in the center from the beginning.

Rather, traces that kasuru and remain may reconnect later through records, media, rediscovery, or changes in receiver-side history.

In HSS terms, the observation point is not whether a symbol immediately sasaru, but how, where, and when it connects, remains, disappears, or returns later.

## What HSS Made Visible

- “Sasaru” can be observed not as simple novelty, but as high-precision connection to existing layered history.
- “Kasuru” can be observed as touching the edge of a reconnectable area and leaving a trace.
- This report makes visible that strong immediate reaction and future reconnectability are different connection states.

## What Remains Unseen / Pending

- Something that leaves no trace, no record, no memory, and no later reconnection almost disappears from HSS observation.
- Therefore, this report can only treat “things that seemed not to connect but left some trace,” not absolute non-connection.
- This is an observation limit, not a judgment on HSS itself.

## Connection-Confirmation States

- connection confirmed: Sasaru and kasuru can be separated as different connection states.
- Blue residuals present: Kasuru traces may remain as discomfort, memory, fragments, records, or later reinterpretation.
- reconnectable area present: Later reconnection may occur through records, rediscovery, context changes, or changes in the receiver-side history.
- out-of-scope layer / insufficient traces: Completely traceless non-connection is difficult to observe with HSS.
